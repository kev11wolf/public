📅  Friday, April 10, 2026  —  Augusta National Golf Club

Round Two: Rory Makes History, the Cut Makes Enemies, and Several Rosters Are Now Seeking Grief Counseling.

 

Good evening, friends. Rory McIlroy posted a 7-under 65 on Friday and now stands at 12-under par — the largest 36-hole lead in the history of the Masters. Six strokes. Patrick Reed and Sam Burns are the nearest company at 6-under, which is a polite way of saying they are watching from the lobby. Tommy Fleetwood, Justin Rose, and Shane Lowry sit at 5-under and are the most credible threats, though they will need the weekend to do something extraordinary in a hurry.

The cut landed at 4-over and was not gentle. Thirty-seven players are heading home, including Bryson DeChambeau, who completed two bunker disasters this week by making triple bogey at 18 on Friday. Robert MacIntyre, Akshay Bhatia, and several other names that were drafted with great optimism also did not survive — a fact that has direct and painful consequences for the pool. In more cheerful news, Haotong Li spent Thursday night “living in the toilet” with a stomach illness, then went out Friday and shot 69 to sit at 4-under. That is either admirable determination or a referendum on everyone else’s week.

A few things worth noting heading into the weekend:

🏆  Historic lead:  McIlroy’s six-shot margin is the largest 36-hole advantage in Masters history. The field would need Saturday to produce a miracle and Rory to produce a disaster simultaneously.

 

⚠️  Bunker disasters:  DeChambeau needed three shots to escape a bunker at 11 in Round 1, then tripled 18 on Friday to seal a missed cut. Augusta’s sand had no mercy this week.

 

🤓  Warrior of the week:  Haotong Li, sick overnight, still shot 69 and sits at ‑4 in genuine contention heading into the weekend.

 

📊  Saturday watch:  Fleetwood, Rose, and Lowry at ‑5 are the best realistic threats, with Cameron Young at ‑4 after a Friday 65. Someone will make a charge — the question is whether there is enough tournament left to catch Rory.
