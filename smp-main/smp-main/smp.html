      📣 Special Announcement

      The Committee extends a distinguished acknowledgment to one of our most enduring members, Mr. Jordan Barnes Cole of Wasilla.

      Mr. Cole has, with admirable consistency, maintained a position within the Top 50 of the Official World Golf Ranking, thereby ensuring his recurring presence at the Masters Tournament—an achievement we regard with both respect and mild astonishment.

      In a further demonstration of his devotion to the game, Mr. Cole has seen fit to name one of his children Ludvig, in tribute to one of golf’s most promising young talents. The Committee notes this decision as both bold and, pending future results, potentially prophetic.

      We extend our best wishes to Mr. Cole for the coming season. Should fortune favor him this year, we trust any celebration will be conducted with the same decorum expected at Augusta—though we would not object to the inclusion of a proper Alaskan meal, should Mr. Cole choose to honor his home region at the table—albeit perhaps with slightly more enthusiasm from Ludvig and the rest of the Cole family.

      📋 Pool Scoring Rules

      ⛳  Scoring

      Your top four golfers count throughout the tournament. Those four lowest cumulative scores make up your team total as the week unfolds.

      If a player misses the cut, that player is assigned +10 for each remaining round. In practical terms, that means +10 on Friday if needed and +20 across the weekend, so they are effectively out of contention for your counting four.

      🏁  Tiebreaker

      If there is a tie for first place, the tiebreaker is applied in this order:

      1) Whoever picked the Masters winner.

      2) The lower score from your 5th-best scorer.

      3) The lower score from your 6th-best scorer.

      4) If it is still tied, the pot is split 50/50.

      📸 Augusta Ace History

      Ross Somerville (long-lost relative of our tournament hosts, the Sommervilles) made the first hole-in-one in Masters history during the 1934 tournament, the very first year the event was played at Augusta National. The Canadian amateur aced the 16th hole, giving the Somerville name a permanent place in Augusta history from the start.

      Somerville was one of the leading amateurs of his era, and his ace came before the tournament was even officially known as the Masters Tournament.
