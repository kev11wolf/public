Main Fields of Philosophy and Theology

Metaphysics

Main Ideas and Topics: Metaphysics examines the most general features of reality, such as existence, objects and their properties, wholes and their parts, space and time, events, and causation. It addresses fundamental questions like "Why is there something rather than nothing?" and "Of what does reality ultimately consist?" It is divided into general metaphysics (being as such) and specific metaphysics (different kinds of being). Key topics include ontology (concepts like being, becoming, and reality), philosophical cosmology (the essence of the world as a whole), identity (how entities remain the same despite change), and distinctions between particulars and universals. It also explores whether reality consists only of physical things or includes mental and abstract entities, and implications for free will and determinism.

Key Philosophers: Aristotle, Immanuel Kant, Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, Martin Heidegger, David Lewis.

Key Texts: Aristotle's "Metaphysics", René Descartes' "Meditations on First Philosophy", Immanuel Kant's "Critique of Pure Reason", Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz's "Monadology", Martin Heidegger's "Being and Time".

Epistemology

Main Ideas and Topics: Epistemology, or theory of knowledge, studies what knowledge is, how it arises, its limits, and its value. It examines the nature of truth, belief, justification, and rationality, addressing questions like "By what method(s) can one acquire knowledge?" and "How is truth established?" It focuses on declarative knowledge (facts), practical knowledge (skills), and knowledge by acquaintance (direct familiarity). Key areas include the analysis of knowledge (e.g., the justified true belief model and the Gettier problem), sources of knowledge (perception, introspection, memory, inference, testimony), and responses to the regress problem (infinite regress or circular reasoning) via foundationalism (self-justifying sources) and coherentism (beliefs justified by coherence with others). It also deals with philosophical skepticism, which doubts claims to knowledge due to the demand for absolute certainty.

Key Philosophers: Plato, René Descartes, John Locke, Edmund Gettier, W.V.O. Quine.

Key Texts: Plato's "Theaetetus", René Descartes' "Meditations on First Philosophy", John Locke's "An Essay Concerning Human Understanding", Edmund Gettier's "Is Justified True Belief Knowledge?", W.V.O. Quine's "Epistemology Naturalized".

Ethics

Main Ideas and Topics: Ethics, or moral philosophy, investigates what constitutes right conduct, the moral evaluation of character traits and institutions, and the standards of morality for living a good life. It addresses questions like "Are moral obligations relative?" and "What gives life meaning?" It is divided into meta-ethics (nature and sources of morality, meaning of ethical concepts, moral relativism), normative ethics (theories distinguishing right from wrong, obligations and rights), and applied ethics (consequences in specific situations like workplace or medical decisions). Contemporary normative ethics includes consequentialism (actions judged by outcomes, e.g., utilitarianism maximizing happiness), deontology (duties like not lying, regardless of consequences), and virtue ethics (actions conforming to virtuous character traits like generosity).

Key Philosophers: Aristotle, Immanuel Kant, John Stuart Mill, Friedrich Nietzsche, Elizabeth Anscombe.

Key Texts: Aristotle's "Nicomachean Ethics", Immanuel Kant's "Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals", John Stuart Mill's "Utilitarianism", Friedrich Nietzsche's "On the Genealogy of Morality", Elizabeth Anscombe's "Modern Moral Philosophy".

Logic

Main Ideas and Topics: Logic studies correct reasoning, distinguishing good arguments from bad ones. It is divided into formal logic (using symbolic languages to examine argument structure for validity) and informal logic (non-formal criteria like content and context). Key forms include deductive arguments (valid if premises ensure conclusion, e.g., modus ponens: "If P then Q; P; therefore Q"), inductive reasoning (generalizations from cases, e.g., "All observed ravens are black, so all ravens are black"), and abductive reasoning (best explanation, e.g., diagnosing disease from symptoms). It also covers fallacies (formal errors in structure; informal errors in content/context).

Key Philosophers: Aristotle, Gottlob Frege, Bertrand Russell, Ludwig Wittgenstein, W.V.O. Quine.

Key Texts: Aristotle's "Organon", Gottlob Frege's "Begriffsschrift", Bertrand Russell's "Principia Mathematica", Ludwig Wittgenstein's "Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus", W.V.O. Quine's "Methods of Logic".

Aesthetics

Main Ideas and Topics: Aesthetics studies the nature and appreciation of beauty and other aesthetic properties like the sublime, encompassing critical reflection on art, culture, and nature. It addresses whether beauty is objective or subjective, the nature of aesthetic experiences and judgments, the essence of works of art and their creation. It is broader than philosophy of art, including natural beauty.

Key Philosophers: Plato, Immanuel Kant, Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel, Arthur Schopenhauer, John Dewey.

Key Texts: Plato's "The Republic" (Book X on mimēsis), Immanuel Kant's "Critique of Judgment", Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel's "Lectures on Aesthetics", Arthur Schopenhauer's "The World as Will and Representation", John Dewey's "Art as Experience".

Political Philosophy

Main Ideas and Topics: Political philosophy inquires into fundamental principles governing political systems and societies, examining the nature and purpose of government, its forms, and legitimate use of power (vs. violence). It addresses distribution of power, social goods, legal rights, and topics like justice, liberty, equality, sovereignty, and nationalism. It differs from political science by focusing on normative matters and is often a subfield of ethics. Influential schools include liberalism, conservatism, socialism, and anarchism.

Key Philosophers: Plato, Aristotle, Thomas Hobbes, John Rawls, Karl Marx.

Key Texts: Plato's "The Republic", Aristotle's "Politics", Thomas Hobbes' "Leviathan", John Rawls' "A Theory of Justice", Karl Marx's "The Communist Manifesto".

Philosophy of Mind

Main Ideas and Topics: The philosophy of mind studies mental phenomena and their relation to the physical world, including conscious and unconscious states like beliefs, desires, intentions, feelings, sensations, and free will. It addresses the mind-body problem (how mind and matter relate), with views like materialism (matter fundamental), idealism (mind fundamental), dualism (distinct entities), and functionalism (mental states as causal roles). It also explores the hard problem of consciousness (how physical brains produce subjective experiences).

Key Philosophers: René Descartes, John Locke, David Hume, Gilbert Ryle, Daniel Dennett.

Key Texts: René Descartes' "Meditations on First Philosophy", John Locke's "An Essay Concerning Human Understanding", David Hume's "A Treatise of Human Nature", Gilbert Ryle's "The Concept of Mind", Daniel Dennett's "Consciousness Explained".

Philosophy of Language

Main Ideas and Topics: The philosophy of language examines the nature and function of language, including meaning, reference, and truth. It answers how words relate to things, how language affects thought and understanding, and how sentences get meaning. It divides into truth-conditional theories (formal conditions for truth) and speech act theory (suitability of use). It relates to logic and linguistics, prominent in analytic philosophy.

Key Philosophers: Gottlob Frege, Bertrand Russell, Ludwig Wittgenstein, Noam Chomsky, J.L. Austin.

Key Texts: Gottlob Frege's "On Sense and Reference", Bertrand Russell's "On Denoting", Ludwig Wittgenstein's "Philosophical Investigations", Noam Chomsky's "Syntactic Structures", J.L. Austin's "How to Do Things with Words".

Philosophy of Science

Main Ideas and Topics: The philosophy of science reflects on science's fundamental concepts, assumptions, and problems, distinguishing it from pseudoscience. It examines methods, how they produce knowledge, underlying assumptions, and implications. Questions include "What counts as an adequate explanation?", "Are scientific laws mere regularities?", and "Can special sciences reduce to general ones?" It divides into philosophy of natural and social sciences, overlapping with metaphysics and epistemology.

Key Philosophers: Karl Popper, Thomas Kuhn, Paul Feyerabend, Imre Lakatos, Larry Laudan.

Key Texts: Karl Popper's "The Logic of Scientific Discovery", Thomas Kuhn's "The Structure of Scientific Revolutions", Paul Feyerabend's "Against Method", Imre Lakatos' "The Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes", Larry Laudan's "Progress and Its Problems".

Existentialism

Main Ideas and Topics: Existentialism emphasizes individual existence, freedom, and choice, often in contrast to essentialism or determinism. It explores themes like authenticity, absurdity, anxiety, and the human condition, rejecting traditional religious or metaphysical absolutes. It arose in the 19th-20th centuries, influenced by phenomenology and skepticism.

Key Philosophers: Søren Kierkegaard, Friedrich Nietzsche, Jean-Paul Sartre, Martin Heidegger, Simone de Beauvoir, Fyodor Dostoevsky.

Key Texts: Søren Kierkegaard's "Fear and Trembling", Friedrich Nietzsche's "Thus Spoke Zarathustra", Jean-Paul Sartre's "Being and Nothingness", Martin Heidegger's "Being and Time", Simone de Beauvoir's "The Ethics of Ambiguity", Fyodor Dostoevsky's "The Brothers Karamazov".

Fyodor Dostoevsky's Beliefs and Field: Dostoevsky's philosophical views are rooted in humanism, emphasizing the human condition in troubled political, social, and spiritual atmospheres. He explored themes of good and evil, free will, redemption through suffering, the search for meaning, the nature of faith vs. atheism, and criticism of rationalism and nihilism. His works suggest that true goodness comes through suffering and that lack of faith leads to societal destruction. Influenced by Orthodox Christianity, he wrestled with questions of individual freedom, responsibility, and the existence of God.

Phenomenology

Main Ideas and Topics: Phenomenology investigates the structures of experience and consciousness, focusing on how things appear to us (the "things themselves"). It uses phenomenological reduction (epochē) to bracket assumptions about reality, aiming for rigorous description of appearances. It emphasizes first-person experience and essences, influencing existentialism and hermeneutics.

Key Philosophers: Edmund Husserl, Martin Heidegger, Maurice Merleau-Ponty, Max Scheler, Jean-Paul Sartre.

Key Texts: Edmund Husserl's "Logical Investigations", Martin Heidegger's "Being and Time", Maurice Merleau-Ponty's "Phenomenology of Perception", Max Scheler's "Formalism in Ethics and Non-Formal Ethics of Values", Jean-Paul Sartre's "The Transcendence of the Ego".

Christian Theology

Main Ideas and Topics: Christian theology is the study of Christian belief and practice, concentrating on the texts of the Old Testament and New Testament, as well as Christian tradition. It involves biblical exegesis, rational analysis, and argument to understand Christian tenets, make comparisons with other traditions, defend Christianity against objections, facilitate church reforms, propagate Christianity, or address present situations using Christian resources. It is defined by Thomas Aquinas as constituted by what is taught by God, teaches of God, and leads to God, encompassing theophanic revelation, systematic study of divine nature and religious belief, and the spiritual path. It may also involve rational study of doctrines, coherence of biblical and traditional language, and claims.

Key Theologians/Philosophers: Thomas Aquinas, Augustine of Hippo, Tertullian, Boethius, Friedrich Schleiermacher.

Key Texts: "The City of God" by Augustine, "Summa Theologica" by Thomas Aquinas, "The Sentences" by Peter Lombard, "Institutes of the Christian Religion" by John Calvin, "On the Trinity" by Augustine.

Islamic Theology (Kalam)

Main Ideas and Topics: Islamic theological discussion, known as Kalam, parallels Christian theological discussion but does not hold the leading place in Muslim thought that theology does in Christianity. It involves investigation and elaboration of Sharia or Fiqh, with Kalam focusing on rational defense of Islamic doctrines, cosmology, and attributes of God. It addresses revelation, the nature of divinity, and interaction with the natural world, using philosophical and argumentative methods to explain and critique religious topics.

Key Theologians/Philosophers: Al-Ghazali, Ibn Sina (Avicenna), Al-Farabi, Malik ibn Anas, Al-Ash'ari.

Key Texts: "The Incoherence of the Philosophers" by Al-Ghazali, "Tahafut al-Falasifah" by Al-Ghazali, "Metaphysics" by Ibn Sina, "Ihya Ulum al-Din" by Al-Ghazali, "Al-Muwatta" by Malik ibn Anas.

Jewish Theology

Main Ideas and Topics: Jewish theology has developed within the context of the Jewish community and synagogue, particularly after the destruction of the Second Temple, emphasizing rabbinical discussion of Jewish law and Midrash (rabbinic biblical commentaries). It is linked to ethics, influencing behavior, and involves reflection on divinity, revelation, and the nature of God without a centralized political authority. It uses analysis to understand religious topics, defend traditions, and explore divine interaction with the world.

Key Theologians/Philosophers: Maimonides, Philo of Alexandria, Saadia Gaon, Martin Buber, Emil Fackenheim.

Key Texts: "Guide for the Perplexed" by Maimonides, "The Mishneh Torah" by Maimonides, "Emunot ve-De'ot" by Saadia Gaon, "I and Thou" by Martin Buber, "The Star of Redemption" by Franz Rosenzweig.

Hindu Theology/Philosophy (Vedanta)

Main Ideas and Topics: Hindu philosophy includes numerous traditions of speculation on the universe, God (as Brahman, Paramatma, Ishvara, or Bhagavan), and the atman (soul), under the term darśana ('viewpoint'). The most influential is Vedanta and its sub-schools, each presenting theories of Ishvara (Supreme Lord). Vaishnava theology classifies manifestations of gods; Shaivism includes dualistic and non-dualistic strands; Shakta traditions posit a feminine deity as ultimate. It involves philosophical analysis of divinity, ultimate reality, and ethical implications, using rational and experiential methods.

Key Theologians/Philosophers: Adi Shankara, Ramanuja, Madhva, Ramananda, Swami Vivekananda.

Key Texts: "Brahma Sutras" by Badarayana, Upanishads (various), "Bhagavad Gita", "Vedanta Sutras with Commentary" by Adi Shankara, "The Gospel of Ramakrishna" by Ramakrishna.

Buddhist Theology/Philosophy

Main Ideas and Topics: Buddhism lacks a conception of a Creator God, so some prefer "Buddhist philosophy" over "theology," but theology is applicable to discussions on Buddhahood, ultimate reality, and divinity. It includes "buddhology," studying the nature of a Buddha, with Mahayana's Trikaya doctrine (three Buddha bodies) central. It involves investigation of the world, nature of reality, and paths to enlightenment, using philosophical theories without assuming a theos, addressing epistemology, revelation, and ethics.

Key Theologians/Philosophers: Nagarjuna, Asanga, Vasubandhu, Louis de La Vallée-Poussin, Jose Ignacio Cabezon.

Key Texts: "Mulamadhyamakakarika" by Nagarjuna, "Abhidharmakosha" by Vasubandhu, "Lotus Sutra", "Milinda Panha", "The Fundamental Wisdom of the Middle Way" by Nagarjuna.

Humanism (Renaissance Theology)

Main Ideas and Topics: Emphasis on human potential, classical learning, reform in education and church, free will, and ad fontes (back to sources). Humanism combines Christian thought with classical traditions, promoting tolerance, education, and internal reform.

Key Philosophers/Theologians: Desiderius Erasmus, Thomas More, Petrarch, Lorenzo Valla, Marsilio Ficino.

Key Texts: "In Praise of Folly" by Erasmus, "Utopia" by More, "On Pleasure" by Valla, "Theologia Platonica" by Ficino, "Letters" by Petrarch.

Desiderius Erasmus's Beliefs and Field: Erasmus was a Dutch Renaissance humanist, theologian, and scholar who advocated for church reform without schism, emphasized inner piety over external rituals, and promoted the study of classical texts and the Bible in original languages. His field bridges theology and philosophy, focusing on Christian humanism, free will (against Luther's predestination), pacifism, and the compatibility of faith and reason. He believed in an individual's capacity for self-improvement through education and critiqued church corruption while advocating tolerance and biblical humanism.
